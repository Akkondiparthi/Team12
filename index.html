IoT Sensor Coverage
How industry optimises this parameter — step by step
📌 WHAT THIS PARAMETER MEANS
IoT Sensor Coverage measures the percentage of plant assets — equipment, pipelines, utilities, and environmental points — that are connected to sensors feeding real-time data into the digital platform. Higher coverage means more visibility, faster alerts, and better data for analytics and AI models. In a paper and pulp mill, sensors typically track temperature, pressure, vibration, flow rate, consistency, chemical concentration, and emissions.
1
Asset Audit & Prioritisation
Start by taking stock of every asset in the mill — machines, motors, pumps, pipelines, and utility systems. Map out which assets currently have no sensor coverage. Then rank them by criticality: which ones cause the most downtime when they fail, consume the most energy, or pose the highest safety or quality risk. Industry practice is to focus the first wave of sensor deployment on the top 20% of assets that drive 80% of the risk.
FMEA Analysis Asset Criticality Ranking Coverage Gap Map
2
Right Sensor Selection for Harsh Environments
Paper mills are tough environments — high humidity, steam, vibration, and chemicals. Selecting the wrong sensor leads to frequent failures and unreliable data. Industry standard is to use IP67 or IP69K-rated sensors for wet-end areas. Vibration and temperature sensors go on rotating equipment. Smart meters are installed on every major power and steam circuit. Inline analyser probes measure chemical consistency, Kappa number, and brightness at key process points. Each sensor type must be matched to the process variable it will track.
IP67/IP69K Rated Sensors Vibration & Temp Probes Smart Power Meters Inline Process Analysers
3
Network Infrastructure Build-out
Sensors are only as useful as the network that carries their data. In a paper mill, traditional wired networks are difficult to extend to all corners of the plant. Industry best practice is to deploy an industrial Wi-Fi mesh network across all zones — including wet-end areas that often have coverage gaps. Edge gateways are installed at zone level to collect sensor readings locally and batch-transmit to the central platform, reducing latency and network load. Protocol converters bridge older PLCs and legacy equipment that use Modbus or PROFIBUS with modern MQTT- or OPC-UA-based IoT platforms.
Industrial Wi-Fi Mesh Edge Gateways Protocol Converters (OPC-UA)
4
Integration, Commissioning & Calibration
Once sensors are installed, they must be connected to the IoT platform and validated before operations teams can trust the data. Industry practice involves a 4–6 week parallel monitoring phase where sensor readings are compared against manual measurements and lab samples to catch calibration drift or installation errors. Real-time alerting thresholds are configured — for example, a temperature alert fires if a motor bearing exceeds 85°C. Operations and maintenance teams are trained on reading dashboards and responding to alerts rather than doing manual rounds.
Parallel Monitoring Period Alert Threshold Configuration Operator Training
5
Scale, Optimise & Build Intelligence
Once the core assets are covered, coverage is progressively expanded to secondary equipment. The real value of high sensor coverage is unlocked when data density is sufficient to train predictive models. At this stage, companies build digital twins — virtual replicas of key equipment — using the historical sensor data to simulate failure scenarios and optimise setpoints without disrupting real production. Sensor coverage is reviewed quarterly, and blind spots discovered during operations are filled in each review cycle.
